Rabbit Health Guide: GI Stasis, Dental Disease, and Why Rabbits Hide Everything

March 6, 20266 min readBy VetGPT

There's a condition that kills rabbits quietly, quickly, and with very little warning. It doesn't announce itself with dramatic symptoms. There's no vomiting. No obvious pain response. No clear moment where you know something is wrong.

Instead, your rabbit gets still. A little quieter. Less interested in food. Sitting in their corner a bit more than usual.

That's GI stasis. And it can take a rabbit in 24 to 48 hours.

If you own a rabbit, understanding this condition — and building the observational habits to catch it early — may be the single most important thing you can do.

What Is GI Stasis?

The rabbit digestive system is designed to be in nearly constant motion. A rabbit's gut moves food through continuously, propelled by regular eating, exercise, and gut motility. GI stasis is what happens when this motion slows or stops.

When the gut stops moving, gas accumulates and cannot pass. The bacteria in the cecum — normally balanced — begin to overproduce or shift to harmful species. Toxins can be absorbed from the stagnant gut contents. The rabbit stops eating because they feel uncomfortable, which further slows the gut. The spiral deepens fast.

The causes of GI stasis include: inadequate fiber (not enough hay), stress, pain from another condition, dehydration, ingested hair or foreign material, and sometimes no identifiable cause at all.

Why This Is So Hard to Catch

Rabbits are prey animals. In the wild, a sick rabbit is a dead rabbit. The evolutionary pressure to appear normal has produced animals who are extraordinarily good at concealing illness.

A rabbit in early GI stasis may look like a rabbit having a slow afternoon. The early signs are subtle: slightly fewer droppings, slightly smaller droppings, slightly less interest in food. Maybe they're sitting hunched instead of flopped. Maybe they're not coming out to greet you the way they usually do.

These signs require a baseline to recognize. If you don't know what your rabbit's normal dropping output looks like, you can't recognize when it's less than normal. This is why observational logging for rabbits isn't optional. It's lifesaving.

The 24-Hour Rule: If your rabbit has not eaten in 12–24 hours, contact your vet immediately. If your rabbit has produced no droppings in 12 hours, contact your vet immediately. Don't wait for other symptoms. GI stasis moves fast, and the earlier intervention begins — gut motility medications, subcutaneous fluids, pain management — the better the outcome.

Rabbit Dental Disease: The Hidden Epidemic

GI stasis gets the most attention in rabbit health discussions, but dental disease may be even more prevalent. Rabbit teeth grow continuously throughout their lives. The cheek teeth wear down through the grinding motion of eating hay. When this balance is disrupted, teeth overgrow, develop sharp spurs, and cause lacerations of the tongue and cheeks.

Here's the insidious part: you cannot see a rabbit's cheek teeth. They're located far back in the mouth, inaccessible to examination at home. A rabbit with severe dental disease causing daily pain will often eat normally — because the alternative is not eating.

Signs of dental disease are often indirect: weight loss, preference for softer foods, reduced food intake, wetness around the chin, and behavioral changes. Annual dental exams under sedation are the only way to assess cheek tooth health.

What to Monitor Every Day

📋 Daily Monitoring

  • Droppings — assess volume, size, shape, and consistency. Changes in quantity or quality are the earliest GI stasis warning sign.
  • Eating — is your rabbit eating hay? Hay should be 80%+ of the diet and available at all times.
  • Activity — know your rabbit's normal activity patterns. A rabbit who is unusually still warrants attention.
  • Posture — a hunched posture with teeth grinding (bruxism) indicates pain. A flopped, relaxed rabbit is normal; a hunched, tense rabbit is different.
  • Water intake — normal consumption, or notably less? Dehydration accelerates GI issues.
  • Cecotropes — are they being consumed? Uneaten cecotropes stuck to fur indicate the rabbit isn't able to reach them or isn't interested in food.

📅 Weekly and Monthly

  • Weight — weigh monthly. A rabbit who is losing weight quietly — perhaps because dental disease makes eating painful — will not announce it.
  • Grooming and coat condition — well-maintained coat? Any bald patches, flaky skin, or ear mites (brown waxy debris in ears)?
  • Urine scalding — wet, irritated fur around the hindquarters warrants vet attention.

Finding an Exotic Vet Who Knows Rabbits

Rabbits are exotic animals medically. A dog-and-cat general practice vet may or may not have the training to properly assess a rabbit's dental health, manage GI stasis, or safely anesthetize a rabbit for procedures. Seek out a vet with documented rabbit or exotic animal experience. The House Rabbit Society has a vet directory resource.

Establish this relationship before you need it urgently. In a GI stasis emergency, calling a vet you already know — who already knows your rabbit's history — is a very different experience than cold-calling at midnight.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is GI stasis in rabbits?

GI stasis is when the rabbit's digestive system slows or stops moving. Gas accumulates, gut bacteria shift, and the rabbit stops eating — deepening the spiral. It can take a rabbit in 24–48 hours and often presents with only subtle early signs: fewer droppings, slight reduction in appetite, or unusual stillness.

How do I know if my rabbit has GI stasis?

The 24-hour rule: if your rabbit has not eaten or produced droppings in 12 hours, contact your vet immediately. Don't wait for other symptoms to confirm. GI stasis moves fast and early intervention dramatically improves outcomes.

Why do rabbits hide illness?

Rabbits are prey animals. In the wild, a sick rabbit is targeted by predators. They evolved to appear normal until they physically cannot — which means a rabbit can be seriously ill for days before an untrained eye notices anything wrong.

Do rabbits need exotic vet care?

Yes. Rabbits are exotic animals medically. A general practice vet may not have training to assess rabbit dental health, manage GI stasis, or safely anesthetize a rabbit. Seek a vet with documented rabbit or exotic animal experience. The House Rabbit Society has a vet directory.
