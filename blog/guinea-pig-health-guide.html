Guinea Pigs Are Experts at Hiding Illness — Here's What to Watch For

March 10, 2026 7 min read By VetGPT Team

Guinea pigs — cavies — are gentle, social, and surprisingly complex animals. They are also prey species, which means they are hardwired to mask pain and weakness for as long as possible. By the time a guinea pig looks visibly sick, the problem has usually been building for days or even weeks. The owners who catch illness early are the ones who know their pig's normal inside and out — and track when things shift.

This guide covers what healthy looks like, what to track, the warning signs that warrant a vet call, and how to build a sustainable care routine for your cavy.

What a Healthy Guinea Pig Looks Like

Before you can notice when something is wrong, you need a baseline of what's right. A healthy guinea pig is:

  • Alert and curious — reacts to sounds, movement, and the smell of food with visible interest
  • A consistent eater — guinea pigs graze almost constantly; any significant drop in appetite is notable
  • Vocal in predictable ways — wheeks at meal time, purrs when content, chatters teeth when annoyed
  • Producing normal droppings — consistently shaped, dark pellets throughout the day
  • Maintaining stable weight — not losing grams week over week
  • Moving freely — no limping, no reluctance to move, no hunching

What to Track for Your Guinea Pig

Guinea pig health tracking doesn't have to be complicated. But the data points you log consistently will be exactly what your vet asks for when something goes wrong.

📅 Weekly Tracking Essentials

  • Weight — use a kitchen scale accurate to 1g. Log it every week, same day, same time. A loss of 50g+ over two weeks is a vet call.
  • Appetite — is your pig finishing their hay, pellets, and fresh vegetables? Any sudden disinterest is a red flag.
  • Droppings — check daily for changes in shape, size, color, or quantity. Mushy, absent, or very small droppings all signal a problem.
  • Vitamin C intake — note what fresh foods were offered each day. Bell pepper (especially red), leafy greens, and parsley are excellent sources.
  • Coat and skin — look for patches of hair loss, flaky skin, or excessive scratching (fungal infection or mites are common).
  • Eyes and nose — clear, bright eyes; dry nose. Discharge from either is a respiratory warning sign.
  • Teeth — visible incisors should be straight and relatively even. Malocclusion (misaligned teeth) affects eating and is a common hidden issue.

🏥 Vet Visit Frequency

  • Annual wellness exam — even if your pig seems perfectly fine
  • Exotic or small mammal vet preferred — general practice vets vary significantly in guinea pig knowledge
  • Dental check recommended at every visit — tooth problems are the #1 hidden health issue in cavies
  • Weight check at every vet visit and logged in your records

Warning Signs That Mean Call the Vet Today

Guinea pigs don't give you much time once they start showing symptoms. These signs mean do not wait for the next available appointment — call now.

Contact your vet immediately if you notice: Weight loss of 50g or more in two weeks · Complete loss of appetite for more than 24 hours · Labored, noisy, or open-mouth breathing · Discharge from eyes, nose, or mouth · Droppings that are absent, bloody, or dramatically changed in shape · Head tilt or loss of balance · Swollen abdomen · Teeth chattering combined with hunching (pain signal) · Limping or reluctance to bear weight on a limb

Common Health Conditions in Guinea Pigs

Upper Respiratory Infections (URIs)

URIs are among the most common and most dangerous guinea pig illnesses. They progress quickly. Signs include sneezing, nasal discharge, labored breathing, and lethargy. Cold drafts, stress, and contact with sick animals are common triggers. If you suspect a URI, treat it as urgent — respiratory infections in cavies can become life-threatening within days. Your vet will guide you on appropriate treatment; do not attempt to manage this at home with over-the-counter products.

Dental Malocclusion

Guinea pigs have continuously growing teeth — both the visible incisors and the molars in the back that you can't easily see. When teeth don't wear evenly, they overgrow and cause pain, difficulty eating, and eventually weight loss. The tricky part: molar problems are nearly invisible to owners. This is why a vet with a dental scope and small mammal experience is so valuable. Annual dental checks are not optional for cavies.

Vitamin C Deficiency (Scurvy)

Unlike most mammals, guinea pigs cannot synthesize their own vitamin C. They need 10–30mg daily from their diet (more during illness or pregnancy). Deficiency causes joint pain, lethargy, poor coat quality, and eventually serious health decline. Fresh vegetables — especially bell peppers and leafy greens — are your best source. Water-soluble vitamin C supplements degrade quickly; always consult your vet before supplementing.

Skin Conditions: Fungal Infections and Mites

Hair loss in a roughly circular pattern often points to ringworm (a fungal infection, despite the name). Intense scratching and agitation can indicate mites. Both are treatable but require a vet diagnosis — treatments differ, and using the wrong one won't help. Log when you first noticed symptoms and how they've progressed; that timeline helps your vet considerably.

GI Stasis and Bloat

Guinea pigs need a constant flow of fiber through their digestive system — primarily unlimited timothy hay. When gut motility slows or stops (GI stasis), it becomes a medical emergency. Signs include a swollen abdomen, hunching, reduced or absent droppings, and refusal to eat. This is a call-the-vet-immediately situation, not a wait-and-see.

Frequently Asked Questions

How often should I weigh my guinea pig?

Weekly is the standard — same day, same time, same scale. A single reading means little; it's the trend over weeks that tells the story. A loss of 50 grams or more over two consecutive weeks is a veterinary concern even if your pig seems otherwise fine.

How much vitamin C does a guinea pig need daily?

Guinea pigs need approximately 10–30mg of vitamin C per day from their diet, with more required during illness, pregnancy, or stress. Fresh bell peppers (especially red), leafy greens, and parsley are excellent sources. Do not rely solely on water-added supplements — vitamin C degrades quickly in water. Consult your vet for specific recommendations.

What are the most common health problems in guinea pigs?

Upper respiratory infections, dental malocclusion, vitamin C deficiency (scurvy), fungal skin infections (ringworm), and GI issues including bloat. Many of these have subtle early signs — which is exactly why weekly weight tracking and regular vet check-ups matter so much for cavies.

Do guinea pigs need an exotic vet?

They benefit greatly from one. Guinea pigs have species-specific needs that many general practitioners aren't deeply trained in — especially around dental health, which requires specialized equipment to assess properly. Look for a vet who lists small mammals or exotic animals as a specialty.
